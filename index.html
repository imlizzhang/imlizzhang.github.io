    Research Interests

    My research focuses on developing i-Tree Research Suite - HydroPlus to simulate the effects of climate change, urban surface changes, and human activities on the hydrological cycle. This includes assessing impacts on water quality, nutrient loading, and flood risk, as well as evaluating the environmental benefits of trees. My main contributions are:

    1) i-Tree Buffer
    - Developed a temeral model: Stochastic Export Coefficient Model to simulate annual variations in nutrient loading from non-point source pollution. This model provides crucial insights for optimizing nutrient management plans across diverse watersheds by predicting fluctuations in pollutant loads over time, particularly in ungauged basins where empirical data is limited.
    - Developed a spatial model: i-Tree Buffer to identify pollutant loading hotspots in areas vulnerable to climate change. I developed GI algorithms integrated into the tool, allowing it to prioritize vulnerable areas and optimize the placement of GI installations. This approach maximizes the effectiveness of GI in mitigating environmental hazards. By targeting the most affected areas, this work aligns with environmental justice goals, addressing the unequal distribution of pollution and ensuring that marginalized communities receive targeted intervention and support.

    2) i-Tree Hydro - Developed water quality algorisms for the Green Infrastructures devices. Developed algorithms for geoprocessing operations; contributed to code maintenance and debugging.

    3) i-Tree Cool Air - In collaboration with interdisciplinary teams (e.g. Davey Tree), I contributed to the development of i-Tree Cool Air, a model that generates air temperature maps to quantify the cooling effects of urban tree canopies. I developed a method to generate a national temperature map without abrupt changes along Thiessen Polygon boundaries by employing the inverse-distance weighted (IDW) method, using spatially weighted meteorological inputs from multiple weather stations. This approach offers a more accurate temperature distribution. Collaborated with developers and scientists to intergrate this model with the Social Vulnerability data, i-Tree Cool Air helps address urban heat island effects, particularly in the low-income communities with less green space and high impervious surfaces.

    4) Automated Tools for Data Preprocessing - To streamline large-scale environmental simulations, I developed automated tools for downloading, processing, and preprocessing geographic datasets. These tools significantly improve the efficiency of handling vast environmental data, enabling seamless integration of new datasets into i-Tree HydroPlus and expanding its applicability for diverse environmental scenarios.

    Stochastic Export Coefficient Model to Predict Annual Variation in Phosphorus Loading from Diffuse Runoff

    New publication on Journal of Hydrology!

    Sloved the abrupt spatial changes issue of i-Tree Cool Air

    when running the model for the entire U.S. national map, using meteorological data from the nearest weather stations resulted in spatial discontinuities, as the spatial coverage of weather stations was not uniform, causing abrupt changes in data across boundaries. I addressed this by developing a method that automatically selects the nearest multiple weather stations and applies an inverse distance weighting method to spatially smoothen the data. This innovation allowed for seamless environmental simulations across diverse regions, ensuring that local variations were accounted for without losing precision.

    The development of Stochastic Export Coefficient Model

    The Stochastic Export Coefficient Model is created to simulate annual variation in nonpoint source pollution loading for river basins with nutrient management plans. This model is now functioning in HydroPlus.

    Green Infrastructure devices in i-Tree

    creating algorisms for the Green Infrastructures devices, allowing multiple GIs running in parallel.

    Generting Export Coefficient databases via HyDroPlus costs less than 1s

    Bringing in easy to use functionality to create export coefficient databases that were creating with a set of 2 Python scripts. These export coefficient databases provide a key element for the GLRI analysis, and are the first step in the process of creating event mean concentration data.
